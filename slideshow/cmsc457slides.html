In classical physics, particles and waves are distinct.

  • Particles are localized entities with specific positions and momentum.
  • Waves are extended and characterized by properties like wavelength and frequency.

However, in the quantum realm, objects such as electrons, photons, and even large molecules exhibit both particle-like and wave-like behaviors depending on the experimental setup.

One of the most famous demonstrations of wave-particle duality is the double-slit experiment:

The Double-Slit Experiment

One of the most famous demonstrations of wave-particle duality is the double-slit experiment:

  • When electrons pass through two slits without observation, they form an interference pattern, characteristic of wave behavior.

  • When detectors are placed at the slits to observe which slit each electron passes through, the interference pattern disappears, and the electrons behave like particles.

This experiment illustrates that the act of measurement affects the behavior of quantum systems—a key principle in quantum mechanics.

Mathematical Representation:

This experiment illustrates that the act of measurement affects the behavior of quantum systems—a key principle in quantum mechanics.

A particle’s wave-like nature is described by a wavefunction \( \Psi(x, t) \), which determines probability amplitude of finding the particle at position \( x \) and time \( t \).

The square of the wavefunction's magnitude, \( |\Psi(x, t)|^2 \), gives the probability density of the particle's position.

The wavefunction evolves according to the Schrödinger Equation, which describes how the wavefunction changes over time.

The Uncertainty Principle

One of the foundational concepts of quantum mechanics is the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, formulated by Werner Heisenberg in the 1920s.

It states that certain pairs of quantum properties—such as position (\( x \)) and momentum (\( p \))—are in a tradeoff.

  • The more precisely you know one property, the less certain you can be about the other.

This relationship is described by the inequality:

\( \Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \)

(where \( \Delta x \) is the uncertainty in position, \( \Delta p \) is the uncertainty in momentum, and \( \hbar \) is the reduced Planck constant.)

\( \Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2} \)
  • Imagine a seesaw on a playground: one rider can only enjoy the view when the other sits near the ground.

  • Similarly, in the quantum world, if you pinpoint an electron’s location with precision, you lose certainty about how fast it’s moving, and vice versa.
    • How? Our wave-particle duality suggests that electrons behave like waves and particles simultaneously.
      • In this case, we can consider light as composed of small particles of energy called photons.
    • Because you need to shine light on the electron to measure its position (lowering \( \Delta x \)), the light’s photons transfer momentum to the electron, increasing \( \Delta p \).

Although Heisenberg initially applied this principle to position and momentum, it holds for other pairs of complementary quantum properties, such as energy and time - reflecting the fundamental limiting factor of nature.

Applications in Quantum Computing

Wave-particle duality and the uncertainty principle is essential for grasping the behavior of quantum bits (qubits) in quantum computers:

  • Superposition: Qubits can exist in a superposition of states, a direct result of wave-particle duality. Measurement collapses this superposition, limiting direct observation of quantum states.
  • Entanglement: Wave-particle duality and non-locality enable entanglement, where the state of one qubit instantaneously affects the state of another.
  • Quantum Gates: Operations on qubits must account for uncertainty and avoid decoherence, which disrupts superposition and entanglement.
  • Coherence: Qubits must maintain coherence during computations, and the uncertainty principle governs the precision with which certain operations can be performed.
